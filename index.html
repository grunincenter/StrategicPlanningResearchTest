Research Overview: U.S. Law School Landscape

This dashboard presents research compiled on U.S. Law Schools, focusing on their ranking, tier status, and confirmed/potential summer 2025 programming relevant to specific legal fields. The data combines information on top-tier schools with a broader tiered breakdown.

Key findings suggest a strong concentration of relevant summer programs and high-ranking status among the T14 schools, though significant program activity is also noted across all tiers. This analysis supports strategic planning for student outreach, academic collaborations, and program development.

Process

  • Read the Copy of 2022 Strategic Planning PPT - Law School Research Competitive Landscape and visited the websites cited within.
  • Pulled a list of all 199 accredited law schools, and added them to this Workbook - Accredited law schools tab.
  • Pulled all centers and clinics mentioned in the 2022 PPT and added them to the respective Centers and Clinics tabs, including links and details.
  • Checked websites of all centers and clinics mentioned in 2022, and updated any outdated info on the Centers and Clinics tab.
  • Created keywords to use in searching for any new programming related to our subject areas - see Keywords tab.
  • Created GPT prompts to use in searching school websites - see ChatGPT Prompts tab.
  • Divided all 199 law schools between Nick, Nikki or Zvia, and searched all 199 websites to see if any schools have added programming in our topic areas (using keywords + GPT prompts).
  • Noted programming in grid in Accredited law schools tab, and added details into the following tabs: Centers, Clinics, Courses, Events, Case Studies, CLE, Reports, Extracurriculars.
  • **Note:** For all programming, we saved a PDF of the websites so we would have a record of the offerings, in case websites are later edited.
  • After reviewing all 199 websites, we created an Initial Short List in early August 2025 by looking for "social" within the names of Centers and Clinics, including all institutions from 2022 research, and bringing in T14 schools with potentially relevant activities. Generic entrepreneurial programs or programs focused on IP or international transactions were excluded.
  • We created a Late August Short List after meeting with Deborah, focusing on institutional commitment (whether a center or program or institute coordinates all activities), looking at clinic clients for primary keywords, and looking for recurring colloquia/symposia across three academic years (2023-2024; 2024-2025; 2025-2026).
  • Confirmed that all social entrepreneurship clinics were included in our Late August Short List, and checked institutions where Convening attendees teach to confirm their evaluation.
  • Created definitions for Tiers I, II, and III.
  • Nick and Nikki independently reviewed the Late August Short List and sorted schools into Tiers I, II, III, and Don’t Include.
  • Redefined our school categories (strong, substantial, and some institutional commitment) to be based solely on the nature and depth of their institutional commitment to social entrepreneurship (e.g., dedicated centers, required courses, etc.). **US News law school rankings were explicitly excluded as a factor in this categorization.**
  • Visited each law school, center, and clinic website for the schools listed on the Late August Short List, and (without using AI search tools) combed the sites to see what programming, events, extracurriculars etc. we did not include in our initial research.
  • We looked at the 2017 Lit Review data to see what professors are on staff at the shortlisted institutions. We also used a research prompt and generative AI to determine which shortlisted schools have professors on staff actively publishing in social entrepreneurship, impact investing, and/or sustainable development.

Tier Criteria

Strong Institutional Commitment*

Schools in this category demonstrate a high level of institutional commitment to social entrepreneurship, impact investing, and/or sustainable development. This commitment is demonstrated through a dedicated, formal center or institute (or a similarly structured program) that acts as a singular organizing hub for the school's relevant offerings. This center coordinates a comprehensive suite of programming, including clinics, courses, student groups, and recurring events/symposia. This structure often involves cross-campus collaboration (e.g., with a business school) and includes the visible support of institutional leadership (e.g., a dean or associate dean).

Substantial Institutional Commitment**

Schools in this category demonstrate a clear focus on social entrepreneurship, impact investing, and/or sustainable development through specific programming, although they may lack the comprehensive institutional commitment of a school with “strong institutional commitment.” Their engagement is typically part of a broader, more general program (e.g., a general entrepreneurship center) and/or lacks a dedicated institutional commitment. This is typically evidenced by a general entrepreneurship clinic that has worked with social enterprises but does not state it as a core mission, or a business law course with a single related component or module. The institutional commitment to these areas is less clear and not a central priority.

Some Institutional Commitment***

Schools in this category offer programming relevant to social entrepreneurship, impact investing, and/or sustainable development, but this is a secondary focus. Their engagement is typically part of a broader, more general program (e.g., programming that is more generally concerned with entrepreneurship, sustainability, community/international development) and lacks explicit keyword focus in the program's current mission or name. This can be seen in a general entrepreneurship clinic that has worked with social enterprises but does not state it as a core mission, or a business law course with a related component. The institutional commitment to this area is less clear.

Impact Investing Research Limitation*

Although included as a primary keyword and emphasized in our research prompts for Google Gemini and ChatGPT, **“impact investing” rarely appeared** in searches of law schools’, clinics’, and centers’ websites outside of NYU Law. We did find two seminar courses in law schools centered around impact investing (Finance Research Seminar: Impact Investing at Northern Illinois University College of Law offered fall 2025 and Policy Practicum: Accountability for the Unintended Consequences of Impact Investing Stanford Law offered 2017-18). We also found two one-time events that specifically mention impact investing in their titles or descriptions (a 2015 UC Irvine Law workshop “Investing for Environmental Justice in Indian Country: Exploring Social Justice Impact Investing in Tribal Projects” and a 2015 University of Colorado Law symposium “Seeds of Change: Responding to Global Change in a Bottom-Up World”). Additionally, the University of Michigan Law’s International Transactions Clinic states that they take on **“impact investors” as clients**. Beyond these instances, impact investing appears in one center for research description. Pace University’s Sustainable Business Law Hub mentions that they offer courses on **“social impact investing and ESG ratings,”** however, we were not able to find specific details on these courses. While our comprehensive research into law schools did not turn up many instances of “impact investing” in event or program titles or descriptions, we did find at least two centers outside of legal academia with “impact investing” in their names: The ESG & Impact Investing Lab at the University of Chicago’s Harris School of Public Policy and the Wharton (University of Pennsylvania) ESG Initiative & Impact Investing Research Lab.
